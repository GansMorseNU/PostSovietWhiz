## et_0107 · easy · soviet · russia

**Prompt:** A characteristic feature of the late-Soviet 'deficit economy' was:

- **A [✓]** Widespread shortages of consumer goods at state-fixed low prices, long queues, informal blat-based rationing, and a large parallel 'second economy' — all coexisting with household savings accumulating in Sberbank accounts because there was little to buy
- **B [ ]** High consumer-goods prices and low savings, because the ruble floated freely against Western currencies
    - *note:* Consumer prices were administratively fixed and low, not high. The ruble was not freely convertible. The 'overhang' of household savings at Sberbank was a defining feature of the pre-1992 Soviet economy.
- **C [ ]** A fully functioning consumer-goods market in which supply and demand adjusted smoothly through price signals
    - *note:* The Soviet economy did not use price signals to clear consumer markets. Prices were administrative; shortages at those prices — not price adjustment — characterized the system.
- **D [ ]** Import-driven abundance of consumer goods from CMEA partners, with Soviet households reporting satisfaction levels comparable to Western Europe
    - *note:* CMEA trade did not produce consumer-goods abundance in the USSR; Soviet consumer-goods supply lagged Western Europe significantly, and surveys documented widespread dissatisfaction with queues and shortages.

**Explanation:** Kornai's 'shortage economy' concept captures the structural feature: state-fixed prices below market-clearing levels produced persistent excess demand, manifested as queues and shortages rather than price increases. Households accumulated ruble savings they could not easily spend — the monetary 'overhang' that helped drive 1992 hyperinflation once prices were liberalized. Informal allocation through blat (connections), workplace distribution, and the shadow second economy filled the gap. The visibility of queues and empty shelves, especially in the late 1980s as supplies deteriorated further, was politically corrosive in a way Western price-cleared shortages would not have been.
