## Political Transitions

### pt_0026

**Prompt:** What did Ukraine's 2012 Kolesnychenko-Kivalov language law allow?

**Current difficulty:** `medium`
**Era / country:** `2010s_plus` / `ukraine`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Revised after instructor comments: regraded upward and distractors tightened so the item is less guessable by elimination.

**Answer choices:**
1. **It allowed a minority language to be used in official settings in regions where that minority made up at least 10 percent of the population** *(correct)*
2. It made Russian a co-equal second state language across all of Ukraine
   - Wrong-answer note: The law expanded regional official use of minority languages, but it did not make Russian a second state language nationwide.
3. It let regional councils adopt a minority language as the sole language of administration even without meeting a population threshold
   - Wrong-answer note: The law used a numerical threshold and did not authorize regions to displace Ukrainian entirely from administration.
4. It granted official regional language status only where minority speakers formed a majority of the population
   - Wrong-answer note: The threshold was much lower than a majority: 10 percent of the local population.

**Overall explanation:** The law was controversial because it gave Russian and other minority languages greater official standing in places where they were widely spoken. Its passage triggered fierce protests and later became politically important again after Maidan, when debate over repeal fed tensions that Russia exploited.

### et_0020

**Prompt:** What was one major engine of Russia's initial recovery in 1999-2001?

**Current difficulty:** `medium`
**Era / country:** `2000s` / `russia`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Revised after instructor comments: regraded upward and reframed as one major engine rather than a single-cause explanation.

**Answer choices:**
1. **Import substitution after the ruble devaluation made domestic production more competitive** *(correct)*
2. A rapid rebound driven mainly by rising oil prices and export revenues
   - Wrong-answer note: Higher oil prices later mattered a great deal, but the course stresses that the initial post-1998 rebound was driven more immediately by devaluation and import substitution.
3. Large-scale foreign investment into newly privatized Russian manufacturing
   - Wrong-answer note: Foreign investment did not drive the first stage of recovery in 1999-2001.
4. A consumer-credit boom centered on mortgage and retail lending
   - Wrong-answer note: Consumer lending expanded later; it was not the main early driver of the rebound after the 1998 crash.

**Overall explanation:** The 1998 crash was painful, but the cheaper ruble made imports costlier and opened space for domestic producers. Early growth was broader than just oil, reaching industry, agriculture, construction, and services before the oil boom became more central.

### et_0023

**Prompt:** In the lecture's discussion of Russia's 'resource curse,' what was identified as the main political-economy danger?

**Current difficulty:** `medium`
**Era / country:** `2000s` / `russia`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Revised after instructor comments: regraded downward and distractors made less obviously wrong / absolute.

**Answer choices:**
1. **Resource rents let the state postpone reforms and rely less on bargaining with society through broad taxation** *(correct)*
2. Oil exports automatically force a country to abandon all manufacturing
   - Wrong-answer note: The lecture did not present deindustrialization as automatic or total.
3. Resource rents made it easier to preserve inefficient firms and patronage networks without deep restructuring
   - Wrong-answer note: That can happen, but the lecture emphasized the broader state-society problem created when rulers can rely on rents instead of broad taxation and bargaining.
4. Resource dependence mainly forced Moscow to decentralize political power toward oil-producing regions
   - Wrong-answer note: The course stresses that resource wealth tended to strengthen central autonomy, not force major political decentralization.

**Overall explanation:** The lecture stressed that the problem is not only economic volatility. Easy rents can reduce pressure to diversify, reform, and build accountable institutions, because the state can rely heavily on resource income rather than broad taxation of society.

### et_0025

**Prompt:** What was the purpose of creating large Russian state corporations such as the United Aircraft Corporation in the 2000s?

**Current difficulty:** `medium`
**Era / country:** `2000s` / `russia`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Revised after instructor comments: regraded upward and distractors made more logically consistent with state-led modernization.

**Answer choices:**
1. **To consolidate strategic industries and channel state-led modernization** *(correct)*
2. To reorganize strategic sectors into holding companies that could then be privatized rapidly to outside investors
   - Wrong-answer note: These corporations were associated with consolidation and state guidance, not preparation for rapid sell-offs to outside investors.
3. To separate aviation and shipbuilding from defense priorities and leave modernization mainly to market competition
   - Wrong-answer note: The point was more state coordination in sectors treated as strategic, not less.
4. To consolidate fragmented assets while avoiding ongoing state direction over investment and industrial policy
   - Wrong-answer note: These corporations were created precisely as vehicles for continued state direction over strategic sectors.

**Overall explanation:** These new corporations reflected a state-led approach to industrial policy. The idea was to merge fragmented assets, invest in modernization, and build national champions in sectors like aviation, shipbuilding, and technology.

### et_0030

**Prompt:** What problem was the Skolkovo project meant to address in Russia?

**Current difficulty:** `medium`
**Era / country:** `2010s_plus` / `russia`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Revised after instructor comments: regraded upward because the topic is harder in practice than the earlier review pass assumed.

**Answer choices:**
1. **Overdependence on raw-material exports and weak diversification into high-tech sectors** *(correct)*
2. The need to restore collective farms to Soviet levels of output
   - Wrong-answer note: Skolkovo was framed as an innovation and technology initiative, not an agricultural collectivization project.
3. The lack of a national currency after the Soviet collapse
   - Wrong-answer note: Russia had long had the ruble; Skolkovo addressed innovation and diversification, not currency creation.
4. The absence of any domestic oil-refining capacity
   - Wrong-answer note: Russia already had major oil and energy infrastructure; the issue was broader diversification beyond resource dependence.

**Overall explanation:** Associated especially with the Medvedev period, Skolkovo was supposed to help Russia move into fields like IT, energy technology, and biomedicine. Its mixed record highlights how difficult top-down innovation policy can be in a resource-rich, state-led system.

## Foreign Policy

### fp_0011

**Prompt:** What was the main purpose of NATO's Partnership for Peace, launched in 1994?

**Current difficulty:** `medium`
**Era / country:** `1990s` / `both`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Revised after instructor comments: regraded upward; the topic should not be treated as easy merely because it appears clearly in course materials.

**Answer choices:**
1. **To create a cooperative framework that included Central/Eastern European and post-Soviet states without granting immediate Article 5 protection** *(correct)*
2. To admit all former Warsaw Pact states into NATO at once
   - Wrong-answer note: PfP was a flexible partnership framework, not immediate full membership.
3. To replace NATO with the OSCE as Europe's only security institution
   - Wrong-answer note: PfP was created by NATO and operated alongside other institutions.
4. To bar former Soviet republics from any military cooperation with the West
   - Wrong-answer note: PfP did the opposite: it opened channels for cooperation and joint activity.

**Overall explanation:** PfP was meant to blur, rather than harden, a new dividing line in Europe after the Cold War. It offered training and cooperation to countries including Ukraine and Russia while postponing the all-or-nothing question of full NATO membership.

### fp_0012

**Prompt:** In the 1997 NATO-Russia Founding Act, what military restraint did NATO emphasize regarding new members?

**Current difficulty:** `hard`
**Era / country:** `1990s` / `both`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Still worth precision review because exact Founding Act wording matters; regraded upward to hard.

**Answer choices:**
1. **That it had no plan for additional permanent stationing of substantial combat forces in those states** *(correct)*
2. That it would dissolve Article 5 for all members east of Germany
   - Wrong-answer note: Article 5 remained NATO's core collective-defense commitment.
3. That Russia would receive a formal veto over future NATO deployments
   - Wrong-answer note: The act created consultation mechanisms, not a Russian veto.
4. That NATO would stop all military exercises in Central and Eastern Europe
   - Wrong-answer note: NATO did not renounce all exercises in the region.

**Overall explanation:** The Founding Act was part of NATO's effort to reassure Moscow during enlargement by pairing expansion with consultation and limits on permanent force posture in new member states.

### fp_0022

   - Wrong-answer note: Their argument is that the phrase points beyond NATO force posture to the Kremlin's goals toward Ukraine.

### fp_0023

**Prompt:** According to Kuzio, which belief lies at the core of Russian imperial-nationalist arguments about Ukraine?

**Current difficulty:** `hard`
**Era / country:** `2010s_plus` / `both`
**Why it is still in this packet:**
- Retained as hard, but distractors were revised so all options are more logically related to the question.

**Answer choices:**
1. **That Russians, Ukrainians, and Belarusians are branches of one pan-Russian nation rather than fully separate nations** *(correct)*
2. That Ukraine is a real nation but should remain permanently neutral between Russia and the West
   - Wrong-answer note: Kuzio's emphasis is more radical: denial of full Ukrainian nationhood, not simply a preference for neutrality.
3. That Russian speakers in Ukraine naturally prefer annexation by Russia over sovereignty in Ukraine
   - Wrong-answer note: Kuzio focuses on ideological denial of Ukrainian nationhood, not on assuming a single political preference among all Russian speakers.
